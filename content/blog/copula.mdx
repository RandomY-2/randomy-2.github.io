## Why Go Beyond Correlation?

### Pearson Correlation Coefficient

The **population Pearson correlation coefficient** measures the linear relationship between two random variables $X$ and $Y$:

$$
\rho_{X,Y} = \frac{\mathrm{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y}
$$

where $\mathrm{Cov}(X,Y) = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mu_X)(Y - \mu_Y)]$ is the covariance, $\mu_X, \mu_Y$ are the means, and $\sigma_X, \sigma_Y$ are the standard deviations.

Given $n$ paired observations $(x_1, y_1), \ldots, (x_n, y_n)$, the **sample Pearson correlation coefficient** is:

$$
r_{xy} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})^2}\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-\bar{y})^2}}
$$

where $\bar{x}$ and $\bar{y}$ are the sample means. This is an unbiased estimator of the population correlation $\rho$ when the data comes from a bivariate normal distribution.

**Properties:**

* $\rho \in [-1, 1]$
* $\rho = 1$: perfect positive linear relationship
* $\rho = -1$: perfect negative linear relationship
* $\rho = 0$: no linear relationship (but **not** necessarily independence)

### Correlation and the Bivariate Gaussian

The Pearson correlation has a special relationship with the bivariate Gaussian distribution. For a bivariate normal with parameters $(\mu_x, \mu_y, \sigma_x, \sigma_y, \rho)$, the density is:

$$
f(x,y) = \frac{1}{2\pi \sigma_x \sigma_y \sqrt{1-\rho^2}} \exp\!\left( -\frac{1}{2(1-\rho^2)} \left[ \left(\frac{x-\mu_x}{\sigma_x}\right)^2 -2\rho\left(\frac{x-\mu_x}{\sigma_x}\right)\left(\frac{y-\mu_y}{\sigma_y}\right) +\left(\frac{y-\mu_y}{\sigma_y}\right)^2 \right] \right)
$$

For the bivariate Gaussian, $\rho$ **fully characterizes** the dependence structure. This is **not** true for other distributions — a fact that motivates everything that follows.

### Deficiencies of Pearson Correlation

**Anscombe's Quartet** provides a classic demonstration: four datasets with identical correlations but very different relationships (linear, quadratic, outlier-driven, etc.).

More fundamentally, Pearson correlation measures **linear dependence only**. For non-linear relationships — parabolic, sinusoidal, circular — it can return values near zero even when the variables are perfectly dependent.

There is also a direct connection to regression. For simple linear regression $Y_i = \alpha + \beta X_i + \epsilon_i$, the OLS slope estimator is:

$$
\hat{\beta} = r_{xy}\frac{s_y}{s_x}
$$

The correlation coefficient is essentially a **standardized regression slope**. When both variables have the same standard deviation, $r = \hat{\beta}$. This means the limitations of correlation directly translate to limitations of linear regression as a dependence model.

## Sklar's Theorem

**Sklar's Theorem** is the foundational result in copula theory. It states that any multivariate joint distribution can be decomposed into its marginal distributions and a copula that captures the dependence structure.

**Theorem (Sklar, 1959).** Let $H$ be a joint distribution function with marginals $F$ and $G$. Then there exists a copula $C$ such that:

$$
H(x,y) = C(F(x), G(y))
$$

Conversely, if we know the copula and the marginals, we can reconstruct the joint distribution:

$$
C(u,v) = H(F^{-1}(u), G^{-1}(v))
$$

**Intuition:**

* $F(x) = \Pr(X \leq x)$ and $G(y) = \Pr(Y \leq y)$ are marginal CDFs
* $H(x,y) = \Pr(X \leq x, Y \leq y)$ is the joint CDF
* The copula $C$ encodes **only the dependence structure**, completely separated from the marginals

**Simulation via Sklar's Theorem.** To simulate correlated random variables $X$ and $Y$:

1. Generate uniform random variables $U, V$ from the copula $C$ — draw $U \in [0,1]$, then $V \mid U = u$
2. Apply the inverse CDFs: $X = F^{-1}(U)$ and $Y = G^{-1}(V)$

## Properties of Copulas

A copula $C : [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ must satisfy:

**Boundary conditions:**

$$
C(u,0) = C(0,v) = 0
$$

$$
C(u,1) = u, \qquad C(1,v) = v
$$

**2-increasing property:** for any $u_1 \leq u_2$ and $v_1 \leq v_2$,

$$
C(u_2,v_2) - C(u_2,v_1) - C(u_1,v_2) + C(u_1,v_1) \geq 0
$$

The boundary conditions make the copula consistent with uniform marginals on $[0,1]$. The 2-increasing condition ensures that the probability assigned to any rectangle in the unit square is non-negative. Together, a valid copula behaves like a proper joint distribution on the unit square.

### Fréchet–Hoeffding Bounds

Every copula is bounded by the **Fréchet–Hoeffding bounds**, which represent the extreme cases of perfect dependence:

$$
W(u,v) \leq C(u,v) \leq M(u,v)
$$

* **Upper bound** (co-monotonicity): $M(u,v) = \min(u,v)$ — both variables move together as much as possible
* **Lower bound** (counter-monotonicity): $W(u,v) = \max(u+v-1, 0)$ — one variable moves opposite to the other as much as possible

Every valid copula must lie between these two extremes.

### Copula Density Decomposition

The density of a joint distribution can be decomposed into the copula density and the marginal densities:

$$
h(x,y) = c(F(x), G(y)) \cdot f(x) \cdot g(y)
$$

where $c(u,v) = \frac{\partial^2 C(u,v)}{\partial u\,\partial v}$ is the copula density, and $f(x)$, $g(y)$ are the marginal densities. This decomposition makes the separation between dependence and marginals explicit at the density level.

## Copula Families

### Independence Copula

When variables are independent, the copula takes a trivial form:

$$
C(u,v) = \Pi(u,v) = u \cdot v
$$

This corresponds to $H(x,y) = F(x)G(y)$ — the joint distribution is the product of the marginals.

### Gaussian Copula

The **Gaussian copula** is the dependence structure of a multivariate normal distribution, parameterized by a correlation matrix $R$:

$$
C_R(u,v) = \Phi_R\!\left(\Phi^{-1}(u), \Phi^{-1}(v)\right)
$$

where $\Phi_R$ is the bivariate standard normal CDF with correlation matrix $R$, and $\Phi^{-1}$ is the inverse of the univariate standard normal CDF.

**Key properties:**

* Symmetric dependence (upper and lower tails behave identically)
* **No tail dependence** (asymptotically independent in both tails)
* Simple parameter: the correlation coefficient

**Gaussian copula vs. Gaussian distribution:** the marginals do not need to be Gaussian — they can be anything. The copula only imposes that the dependence structure follows a Gaussian pattern in the latent (transformed) space. After applying the inverse CDF transforms, the final joint relationship can be non-linear.

### Student-$t$ Copula

The **Student-$t$ copula** is the dependence structure of a multivariate $t$-distribution, with an additional parameter $\nu$ (degrees of freedom):

$$
C_{\Sigma,\nu}(u,v) = t_{\Sigma,\nu}\!\left(t_\nu^{-1}(u), t_\nu^{-1}(v)\right)
$$

where $t_{\Sigma,\nu}$ is the bivariate $t$-distribution CDF with correlation matrix $\Sigma$ and $\nu$ degrees of freedom, and $t_\nu^{-1}$ is the inverse of the univariate $t$-distribution CDF.

**Key properties:**

* Symmetric dependence
* **Has tail dependence** — stronger dependence in the tails than Gaussian, making it better suited for modeling joint extreme events
* Approaches Gaussian copula as $\nu \to \infty$

### Archimedean Copulas

**Archimedean copulas** are defined using a generator function $\phi$ and offer flexibility for capturing asymmetric tail dependence:

$$
C(u,v) = \phi^{-1}\!\bigl(\phi(u) + \phi(v)\bigr)
$$

where $\phi : [0,1] \to [0,\infty]$ is strictly decreasing, convex, and satisfies $\phi(1) = 0$. The pseudo-inverse handles boundary cases:

$$
\phi^{[-1]}(t) = \begin{cases} \phi^{-1}(t), & 0 \leq t \leq \phi(0) \\ 0, & \phi(0) \leq t < \infty \end{cases}
$$

Different choices of $\phi$ give rise to different copula families with distinct tail behavior.

#### Clayton Copula

The **Clayton copula** exhibits **strong lower tail dependence** and no upper tail dependence — useful for modeling situations where extreme low values tend to co-occur:

$$
\phi(t) = \frac{1}{\alpha}(t^{-\alpha} - 1), \qquad \alpha \in [-1, \infty) \setminus \{0\}
$$

$$
C(u,v) = (u^{-\alpha} + v^{-\alpha} - 1)^{-1/\alpha}
$$

* $\alpha > 0$: positive dependence (higher $\alpha$ = stronger)
* $\alpha \to 0$: independence
* $\alpha = -1$: counter-monotonicity (Fréchet–Hoeffding lower bound)

#### Gumbel Copula

The **Gumbel copula** exhibits **strong upper tail dependence** and no lower tail dependence — useful for modeling situations where extreme high values tend to co-occur:

$$
\phi(t) = (-\ln t)^\alpha, \qquad \alpha \in [1, \infty)
$$

$$
C(u,v) = \exp\!\left(-\left((-\ln u)^\alpha + (-\ln v)^\alpha\right)^{1/\alpha}\right)
$$

* $\alpha = 1$: independence
* $\alpha \to \infty$: co-monotonicity (Fréchet–Hoeffding upper bound)

#### Frank Copula

The **Frank copula** has **no tail dependence** in either tail. It is symmetric and can model both positive and negative dependence:

$$
\phi(t) = -\ln\!\left(\frac{e^{-\alpha t} - 1}{e^{-\alpha} - 1}\right), \qquad \alpha \in (-\infty, \infty) \setminus \{0\}
$$

$$
C(u,v) = -\frac{1}{\alpha}\ln\!\left(1 + \frac{(e^{-\alpha u} - 1)(e^{-\alpha v} - 1)}{e^{-\alpha} - 1}\right)
$$

* $\alpha > 0$: positive dependence
* $\alpha < 0$: negative dependence
* $\alpha \to 0$: independence

### Comparing Copula Families

* **Gaussian & Student-$t$**: symmetric dependence patterns; Student-$t$ adds tail dependence
* **Clayton**: concentration in the lower-left corner (lower tail dependence)
* **Gumbel**: concentration in the upper-right corner (upper tail dependence)
* **Frank**: symmetric with no tail dependence in either direction

## Fitting Copulas to Data

### Pseudo-Observations

To fit a copula, we first transform the data to the unit cube using **pseudo-observations** (also called empirical copula data). The idea is to replace each observation with its normalized rank, removing the marginal distributions and leaving only the dependence structure.

Given original data, rank each variable (smallest = 1, largest = $n$), then compute:

$$
U_i = \frac{R_i}{n+1}
$$

where $R_i$ is the rank. We divide by $n+1$ instead of $n$ to avoid boundary issues at 0 and 1.

Pseudo-observations are **marginal distribution-invariant** — they capture only the dependency structure. Any monotonic transformation of the original data produces the same pseudo-observations (and the same Kendall's $\tau$).

### Parameter Estimation Using Kendall's Tau

**Kendall's tau** ($\tau$) is a rank-based dependence measure that is invariant to monotonic transformations, making it ideal for copula parameter estimation:

$$
\tau = 4 \int_0^1 \int_0^1 C(u,v)\, dC(u,v) - 1
$$

The sample estimator is:

$$
\hat{\tau} = \frac{2}{n(n-1)} \sum_{i < j} \mathrm{sgn}(x_i - x_j) \cdot \mathrm{sgn}(y_i - y_j)
$$

where $\mathrm{sgn}$ is the sign function. The key advantage: Kendall's tau has simple closed-form relationships with copula parameters for many common families.

**Gaussian copula:** given $\tau$, the correlation parameter is:

$$
\rho = \sin\!\left(\tau \cdot \frac{\pi}{2}\right)
$$

**Clayton copula:** given $\tau \in [0, 1)$:

$$
\alpha = \frac{2\tau}{1 - \tau}
$$

**Frank copula:** the relationship involves the Debye function $D_n(x) = \frac{n}{x^n}\int_0^x \frac{t^n}{e^t - 1}\,dt$:

$$
\tau = 1 - \frac{4}{\alpha}\bigl(1 - D_1(\alpha)\bigr)
$$

Since there is no closed-form inverse, numerical root finding is used to recover $\alpha$ from $\hat{\tau}$.

## Vine Copulas

Standard bivariate copulas model the dependence between two variables. For higher dimensions, **vine copulas** decompose the multivariate dependence into a product of bivariate copulas arranged in a tree structure.

### Density Decomposition

Any multivariate density can be decomposed into a product of conditional densities. For three variables:

$$
f(x_1, x_2, x_3) = f_{1 \mid 2,3}(x_1 \mid x_2, x_3) \cdot f_{2 \mid 3}(x_2 \mid x_3) \cdot f_3(x_3)
$$

This decomposition is not unique — there are six valid orderings for three variables, and the number grows combinatorially with dimension. Each ordering corresponds to a different **vine structure**.

### Conditional Densities as Copulas

A key result connects conditional densities to copula densities. Start from the definition of a conditional density and the copula density decomposition:

$$
f_{1 \mid 2}(x_1 \mid x_2) = \frac{f(x_1, x_2)}{f_2(x_2)} = \frac{c_{1,2}(F_1(x_1), F_2(x_2)) \cdot f_1(x_1) \cdot f_2(x_2)}{f_2(x_2)} = c_{1,2}(F_1(x_1), F_2(x_2)) \cdot f_1(x_1)
$$

The marginal $f_2(x_2)$ cancels, leaving the conditional density expressed purely in terms of the copula density and the other marginal.

This extends to higher-order conditionals:

$$
f_{1 \mid 2,3}(x_1 \mid x_2, x_3) = c_{1,2;3}(F_{1 \mid 3}(x_1 \mid x_3), F_{2 \mid 3}(x_2 \mid x_3)) \cdot f_{1 \mid 3}(x_1 \mid x_3)
$$

Here, $c_{1,2;3}$ is a **conditional copula density** that captures the dependence between $X_1$ and $X_2$ **given** $X_3$.

### Constructing the Vine Decomposition

By combining the density decomposition with the copula representation of conditional densities, the three-dimensional joint density becomes:

$$
f(x_1, x_2, x_3) = f_1(x_1) \cdot f_2(x_2) \cdot f_3(x_3) \times c_{1,3}(F_1(x_1), F_3(x_3)) \times c_{2,3}(F_2(x_2), F_3(x_3)) \times c_{1,2 \mid 3}(F_{1 \mid 3}(x_1 \mid x_3), F_{2 \mid 3}(x_2 \mid x_3))
$$

This decomposes into three layers:

* $f_1(x_1) \cdot f_2(x_2) \cdot f_3(x_3)$ — the **marginal densities**
* $c_{1,3}$ and $c_{2,3}$ — **Tree 1**: unconditional pair-copulas linking variables through a central node
* $c_{1,2 \mid 3}$ — **Tree 2**: a conditional pair-copula capturing the residual dependence between $X_1$ and $X_2$ after accounting for $X_3$

This is the essence of a **C-Vine (Canonical Vine)** copula, where one variable (the "center" node) is connected to all others in the first tree. Each subsequent tree models the conditional dependence that remains after accounting for the variables in previous trees. Each pair-copula in the decomposition can be chosen from **any** bivariate copula family (Gaussian, Clayton, Gumbel, Frank, etc.), giving vine copulas enormous flexibility in modeling complex, heterogeneous dependence structures across many variables.
