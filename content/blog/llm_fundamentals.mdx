## Language Models and Transformers

### Statistical Language Models and the N-gram Idea

Before the rise of deep learning, statistical methods were the mainstream approach to language modeling. The core idea is that the probability assigned to a sentence equals the product of the conditional probabilities of each token in the sentence.

For a sentence $S$ composed of words $w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_m$, its probability $P(S)$ can be expressed as:

$$
P(S) = P(w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_m) = P(w_1) \cdot P(w_2 \mid w_1) \cdot P(w_3 \mid w_1, w_2) \cdots P(w_m \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{m-1})
$$

This formula is called the **chain rule of probability**. However, directly calculating all of these conditional probabilities is almost impossible in practice because long word sequences may rarely or never appear in the training corpus.

To make the problem tractable, researchers introduced the **Markov Assumption**: we approximately assume that a word's probability depends only on the previous $n - 1$ words rather than the entire history.

Language models built on this assumption are called **N-gram models**. Here, "N" represents the context window size we consider.

- **Bigram (N=2)**: The simplest case, where a word depends only on the one word before it.

$$
P(w_i \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{i-1}) \approx P(w_i \mid w_{i-1})
$$

- **Trigram (N=3)**: A word depends on the two words before it.

$$
P(w_i \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{i-1}) \approx P(w_i \mid w_{i-2}, w_{i-1})
$$

These probabilities can be estimated with **Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)** from a large corpus:

$$
P(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) = \frac{\text{Count}(w_{i-1}, w_i)}{\text{Count}(w_{i-1})}
$$

N-gram models are simple and historically important, but they have two major limitations:

1. **Data Sparsity**: If a word sequence never appears in the corpus, its estimated probability is 0 unless smoothing is applied.
2. **Poor Generalization**: The model treats words as discrete symbols and cannot naturally capture semantic similarity between words.

### Neural Network Language Models and Word Embeddings

A core weakness of N-gram models is that they treat words as isolated, discrete symbols. To overcome this, researchers turned to neural networks and represented words with continuous vectors:

$$
P(w_t \mid w_{t-n+1}, \ldots, w_{t-1}) = \text{softmax}\!\Big(W_2 \cdot \tanh\!\big(W_1 \cdot [C(w_{t-n+1});\; \ldots;\; C(w_{t-1})] + b_1\big) + b_2\Big)
$$

Where $C(w)$ is the embedding lookup that maps word $w$ to its vector, the semicolons denote concatenation of the $n{-}1$ context embeddings, and $W_1, W_2, b_1, b_2$ are learnable parameters.

Its core idea can be divided into two steps:

1. **Build a semantic space**: Map each word in the vocabulary to a point in a high-dimensional continuous vector space. This point is called a **Word Embedding**. Semantically similar words tend to have vectors that are close together.
2. **Learn the mapping from context to the next token**: Use a neural network to map the vectors of the previous $n-1$ words to a probability distribution over the vocabulary for the next token.

In this architecture, word embeddings are learned jointly with the language modeling objective. As training progresses, the model adjusts each word's vector so that it helps predict the next token more effectively.

Once words are represented as vectors, we can measure relationships between them using **Cosine Similarity**, which calculates the cosine of the angle between two vectors:

$$
\text{CosineSimilarity}(\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{B}) = \frac{\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B}}{\|\mathbf{A}\| \times \|\mathbf{B}\|}
$$

- Cosine value of **1** (0° angle): same direction
- Cosine value of **0** (90° angle): orthogonal
- Cosine value of **-1** (180° angle): opposite direction

### Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN) and LSTM

Although neural network language models introduced word embeddings and improved generalization, their **context window remained fixed** by construction. To go beyond a fixed-length context, **Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs)** introduced an explicit notion of sequence state.

<div align="center">
![Figure 1: RNN Architecture](/figures/llm-fundamental-rrn-architecture.png)
</div>

RNNs maintain a **hidden state** vector that acts as a short-term memory. At each step, the network reads the current input token and combines it with the hidden state from the previous time step to produce a new hidden state:

$$
h_t = \tanh(W_{xh} \cdot x_t + W_{hh} \cdot h_{t-1} + b_h)
$$

$$
y_t = W_{hy} \cdot h_t + b_y
$$

Where $x_t$ is the input at time step $t$, $h_t$ is the hidden state, $y_t$ is the output, and $W_{xh}$, $W_{hh}$, $W_{hy}$ are learnable weight matrices shared across all time steps.

Standard RNNs suffer from a serious practical problem: the **long-term dependency problem**. During backpropagation through long sequences, gradients undergo repeated multiplications, which can cause them to shrink toward zero (**vanishing gradients**) or grow uncontrollably (**exploding gradients**). As a result, the model struggles to learn how early information affects much later outputs.

**Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)** networks were designed to mitigate this problem. Their core innovation is the introduction of a **cell state** together with a set of **gates** that regulate information flow:

<div align="center">
![Figure 2: LSTM Architecture](/figures/llm-fundamental-lstm-architecture.webp)
</div>

- **Forget Gate**: Decides which information to discard from the previous cell state.

$$
f_t = \sigma(W_f \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_f)
$$

- **Input Gate**: Decides which new information from the current input to store, together with a candidate cell state $\tilde{C}_t$.

$$
i_t = \sigma(W_i \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_i)
$$

$$
\tilde{C}_t = \tanh(W_C \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_C)
$$

- **Cell State Update**: The old cell state $C_{t-1}$ is updated by forgetting some old information and adding new candidate information.

$$
C_t = f_t \odot C_{t-1} + i_t \odot \tilde{C}_t
$$

- **Output Gate**: Decides what to expose as the new hidden state based on the current cell state.

$$
o_t = \sigma(W_o \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_o)
$$

$$
h_t = o_t \odot \tanh(C_t)
$$

Here $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function, $\odot$ denotes element-wise multiplication, and $[h_{t-1}, x_t]$ is the concatenation of the previous hidden state and the current input.

The cell state acts as a relatively stable path for information, allowing signals to flow across longer spans more easily than in a vanilla RNN.

### Transformer Architecture Analysis

RNNs and LSTMs process sequences **step by step**: time step $t$ depends on step $t-1$. This limits parallelism, especially during training. The **Transformer** architecture replaced recurrence with **attention**, allowing much more parallel computation across sequence positions during training.

#### Overall Encoder-Decoder Structure

The original Transformer follows a classic **Encoder-Decoder** architecture:

<div align="center">
![Figure 3: Transformer Architecture](/figures/llm-fundamental-transformer-architecture.png)
</div>

1. **Encoder**: Processes the full input sequence and produces a contextual representation for each token.
2. **Decoder**: Generates the target sequence token by token, using both its previous outputs and the encoder's representations.

**Encoder Layer** — each layer applies self-attention followed by a feed-forward network, with residual connections and layer normalization around each sublayer:

$$
Z = \text{LayerNorm}(X + \text{MultiHeadAttn}(Q{=}X,\; K{=}X,\; V{=}X))
$$

$$
\text{EncoderOut} = \text{LayerNorm}(Z + \text{FFN}(Z))
$$

**Decoder Layer** — each layer has three sublayers: masked self-attention over the decoder's own prefix, cross-attention over the encoder output, and a feed-forward network:

$$
Z_1 = \text{LayerNorm}(X + \text{MaskedMultiHeadAttn}(Q{=}X,\; K{=}X,\; V{=}X))
$$

$$
Z_2 = \text{LayerNorm}(Z_1 + \text{MultiHeadAttn}(Q{=}Z_1,\; K{=}\text{EncoderOut},\; V{=}\text{EncoderOut}))
$$

$$
\text{DecoderOut} = \text{LayerNorm}(Z_2 + \text{FFN}(Z_2))
$$

#### From Self-Attention to Multi-Head Attention

Consider the sentence: "The agent learns because **it** is intelligent." When interpreting the word "it," we may pay more attention to "agent" to resolve the reference. **Self-attention** provides a mechanism for a model to compare each token with all other tokens in the sequence and weight them differently.

Self-attention introduces three learned projections for each input token:

- **Query (Q)**: Represents what the current token is looking for.
- **Key (K)**: Represents how each token can be matched.
- **Value (V)**: Represents the information carried by each token.

These vectors are obtained by multiplying the input representation by three learned matrices ($W^Q, W^K, W^V$). The computation proceeds as:

1. **Generate Q, K, V**: Produce projected representations for each token.
2. **Calculate relevance scores**: Compute dot products between a token's query and all keys.
3. **Scale and normalize**: Divide by $\sqrt{d_k}$ so that large dot products do not push the softmax too deeply into saturated regions, then apply softmax to obtain attention weights.
4. **Weighted sum**: Form a weighted sum of the value vectors.

If attention is performed only once, the model may focus on only one type of relationship. **Multi-head attention** addresses this by using multiple learned projections in parallel, allowing different heads to capture different patterns.

**Scaled Dot-Product Attention** — the core operation that computes a weighted sum of values based on query-key similarity:

$$
\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V
$$

**Multi-Head Attention** — each head $i$ independently projects Q, K, and V with its own learned matrices and performs attention. The $h$ outputs are concatenated and projected again:

$$
Q_i = X W_i^Q, \quad K_i = X W_i^K, \quad V_i = X W_i^V
$$

$$
\text{head}_i = \text{Attention}(Q_i,\; K_i,\; V_i)
$$

$$
\text{MultiHead}(Q, K, V) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1, \ldots, \text{head}_h) \; W^O
$$

Where $W_i^Q, W_i^K, W_i^V \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}} \times d_k}$ are per-head projections with $d_k = d_{\text{model}} / h$, and $W^O \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}} \times d_{\text{model}}}$ is the final output projection.

#### Feed-Forward Neural Network

In each encoder and decoder layer, the attention sublayer is followed by a **position-wise feed-forward network (FFN)**. If the attention mechanism aggregates information across the sequence, the FFN then transforms each token representation independently.

The key word is "position-wise": the same FFN is applied to every position separately. In the original Transformer, it consists of two linear transformations with a ReLU activation in between:

$$
\text{FFN}(x) = \text{ReLU}(xW_1 + b_1)W_2 + b_2
$$

#### Residual Connections and Layer Normalization

In each encoder and decoder layer, all submodules are wrapped by an **Add & Norm** operation:

- **Residual Connection (Add)**: Adds the submodule's input $x$ to its output $\text{Sublayer}(x)$. This improves gradient flow and makes deep networks easier to optimize. Formula: $\text{Output} = x + \text{Sublayer}(x)$.
- **Layer Normalization (Norm)**: Normalizes each token representation across its feature dimensions, followed by learned scale and bias parameters. This helps stabilize optimization and improve training dynamics.

#### Positional Encoding

Self-attention by itself does not encode token order. Without positional information, a model cannot distinguish between sequences that contain the same tokens in different orders. **Positional information** solves this by injecting order information into the token representations.

In the **original Transformer paper**, the authors used a fixed sinusoidal positional encoding added to the token embeddings:

$$
PE_{(pos, 2i)} = \sin\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d_{\text{model}}}}\right)
$$

$$
PE_{(pos, 2i+1)} = \cos\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d_{\text{model}}}}\right)
$$

Where $pos$ is the position in the sequence and $i$ is the dimension index.

These encodings are not learned in the original formulation above, although many later Transformer variants use learned positional embeddings or other positional schemes.

### Decoder-Only Architecture

The original Transformer was an encoder-decoder model. Later, many influential language models kept only the decoder stack, producing the **decoder-only** architecture.

Its working mode is **autoregressive generation**:

1. Give the model a starting text prompt (for example, "Agent is").
2. The model predicts the next most likely **token**.
3. Append that token to the input.
4. Predict the next token again.
5. Repeat until a stop condition is reached.

Decoder-only architectures prevent looking into the future through **masked self-attention**. After computing the attention score matrix but before softmax, the model applies a causal mask that replaces scores for future positions with a very large negative number. After softmax, those positions receive probability approximately 0, which prevents the model from attending to information to the right of the current position.

**Advantages of Decoder-Only Architecture:**

- **Unified Training Objective**: The model is trained to predict the next token, which makes it well suited for large-scale self-supervised pretraining on unlabeled text.
- **Simple Structure, Easy to Scale**: Removing the encoder simplifies the architecture. Many modern generative LLMs, including GPT-style and Llama-family models, use decoder-only Transformers.
- **Naturally Suited for Generation**: Autoregressive decoding aligns directly with text generation tasks such as dialogue, writing, and code generation.
