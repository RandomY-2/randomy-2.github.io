## Language Models and Transformers

### Statistical Language Models and the N-gram Idea

Before the rise of deep learning, statistical methods were the mainstream approach to language modeling. The core idea is that the probability assigned to a sentence equals the product of the conditional probabilities of each token in the sentence.

For a sentence $S$ composed of words $w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_m$, its probability $P(S)$ can be expressed as:

$$
P(S) = P(w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_m) = P(w_1) \cdot P(w_2 \mid w_1) \cdot P(w_3 \mid w_1, w_2) \cdots P(w_m \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{m-1})
$$

This formula is called the **chain rule of probability**. However, directly calculating all of these conditional probabilities is almost impossible in practice because long word sequences may rarely or never appear in the training corpus.

To make the problem tractable, researchers introduced the **Markov Assumption**: we approximately assume that a word's probability depends only on the previous $n - 1$ words rather than the entire history.

Language models built on this assumption are called **N-gram models**. Here, "N" represents the context window size we consider.

- **Bigram (N=2)**: The simplest case, where a word depends only on the one word before it.

$$
P(w_i \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{i-1}) \approx P(w_i \mid w_{i-1})
$$

- **Trigram (N=3)**: A word depends on the two words before it.

$$
P(w_i \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{i-1}) \approx P(w_i \mid w_{i-2}, w_{i-1})
$$

These probabilities can be estimated with **Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)** from a large corpus:

$$
P(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) = \frac{\text{Count}(w_{i-1}, w_i)}{\text{Count}(w_{i-1})}
$$

N-gram models are simple and historically important, but they have two major limitations:

1. **Data Sparsity**: If a word sequence never appears in the corpus, its estimated probability is 0 unless smoothing is applied.
2. **Poor Generalization**: The model treats words as discrete symbols and cannot naturally capture semantic similarity between words.

### Neural Network Language Models and Word Embeddings

A core weakness of N-gram models is that they treat words as isolated, discrete symbols. To overcome this, researchers turned to neural networks and represented words with continuous vectors:

$$
P(w_t \mid w_{t-n+1}, \ldots, w_{t-1}) = \text{softmax}\!\Big(W_2 \cdot \tanh\!\big(W_1 \cdot [C(w_{t-n+1});\; \ldots;\; C(w_{t-1})] + b_1\big) + b_2\Big)
$$

Where $C(w)$ is the **embedding lookup** that maps word $w$ to its vector, the semicolons denote concatenation of the $n{-}1$ context embeddings, and $W_1, W_2, b_1, b_2$ are learnable parameters.

Its core idea can be divided into two steps:

1. **Build a semantic space**: Map each word in the vocabulary to a point in a high-dimensional continuous vector space. This point is called a **Word Embedding**. Semantically similar words tend to have vectors that are close together.
2. **Learn the mapping from context to the next token**: Use a neural network to map the vectors of the previous $n-1$ words to a probability distribution over the vocabulary for the next token. In this case, $n-1$ would be the **context window** of the model.

In this architecture, word embeddings are learned jointly with the language modeling objective. As training progresses, the model adjusts each word's vector so that it helps predict the next token more effectively.

Once words are represented as vectors, we can measure relationships between them using **Cosine Similarity**, which calculates the cosine of the angle between two vectors:

$$
\text{CosineSimilarity}(\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{B}) = \frac{\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B}}{\|\mathbf{A}\| \times \|\mathbf{B}\|}
$$

- Cosine value of **1** (0° angle): same direction
- Cosine value of **0** (90° angle): orthogonal
- Cosine value of **-1** (180° angle): opposite direction

### Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN) and LSTM

Although neural network language models introduced word embeddings and improved generalization, their **context window remained fixed** by construction. To go beyond a fixed-length context, **Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs)** introduced an explicit notion of sequence state.

<div align="center">
![Figure 1: RNN Architecture](/figures/llm-fundamental-rrn-architecture.png)
</div>

RNNs maintain a **hidden state** vector that acts as a short-term memory. At each step, the network reads the current input token and combines it with the hidden state from the previous time step to produce a new hidden state:

$$
h_t = \tanh(W_{xh} \cdot x_t + W_{hh} \cdot h_{t-1} + b_h)
$$

$$
y_t = W_{hy} \cdot h_t + b_y
$$

Where $x_t$ is the input at time step $t$, $h_t$ is the hidden state, $y_t$ is the output, and $W_{xh}$, $W_{hh}$, $W_{hy}$ are learnable weight matrices shared across all time steps.

Standard RNNs suffer from a serious practical problem: the **long-term dependency problem**. During backpropagation through long sequences, gradients undergo repeated multiplications, which can cause them to shrink toward zero (**vanishing gradients**) or grow uncontrollably (**exploding gradients**). As a result, the model struggles to learn how early information affects much later outputs.

**Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)** networks were designed to mitigate this problem. Their core innovation is the introduction of a **cell state** together with a set of **gates** that regulate information flow:

<div align="center">
![Figure 2: LSTM Architecture](/figures/llm-fundamental-lstm-architecture.webp)
</div>

- **Forget Gate**: Decides which information to discard from the previous cell state.

$$
f_t = \sigma(W_f \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_f)
$$

- **Input Gate**: Decides which new information from the current input to store, together with a candidate cell state $\tilde{C}_t$.

$$
i_t = \sigma(W_i \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_i)
$$

$$
\tilde{C}_t = \tanh(W_C \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_C)
$$

- **Cell State Update**: The old cell state $C_{t-1}$ is updated by forgetting some old information and adding new candidate information.

$$
C_t = f_t \odot C_{t-1} + i_t \odot \tilde{C}_t
$$

- **Output Gate**: Decides what to expose as the new hidden state based on the current cell state.

$$
o_t = \sigma(W_o \cdot [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_o)
$$

$$
h_t = o_t \odot \tanh(C_t)
$$

Here $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function, $\odot$ denotes element-wise multiplication, and $[h_{t-1}, x_t]$ is the concatenation of the previous hidden state and the current input.

The cell state acts as a relatively stable path for information, allowing signals to flow across longer spans more easily than in a vanilla RNN.

### The Transformer

Even with LSTMs, recurrent models remain inherently sequential — each hidden state $h_t$ depends on $h_{t-1}$, preventing parallelization within a training example. The Transformer eliminates recurrence entirely, relying solely on **self-attention** to model dependencies between positions. Every position can directly attend to every other position in a single layer, reducing the path length between any two tokens to $O(1)$ (compared to $O(n)$ for RNNs). This enables full parallelization and makes learning long-range dependencies far easier.

<div align="center">
![Figure 3: Transformer Architecture](/figures/llm-fundamental-transformer-architecture.png)
</div>

The Transformer uses an encoder-decoder structure. The encoder maps an input sequence $(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ into continuous representations $z = (z_1, \ldots, z_n)$. The decoder generates an output sequence $(y_1, \ldots, y_m)$ auto-regressively, one token at a time.

The **encoder** is a stack of $N = 6$ identical layers. Each layer has two sub-layers: (1) a multi-head self-attention mechanism and (2) a position-wise fully connected feed-forward network. A residual connection wraps each sub-layer, followed by layer normalization — the output of each sub-layer is $\text{LayerNorm}(x + \text{Sublayer}(x))$. To facilitate these residual connections, all sub-layers and embedding layers produce outputs of dimension $d_{\text{model}} = 512$.

The **decoder** is also a stack of $N = 6$ identical layers, but each decoder layer has a third sub-layer inserted between the self-attention and the feed-forward network: a multi-head attention layer that attends over the encoder's output (cross-attention). This is what allows the decoder to incorporate information from the input sequence. Additionally, the self-attention sub-layer in the decoder is **masked** — positions are prevented from attending to subsequent positions by setting those scores to $-\infty$ before softmax. This masking, combined with the fact that output embeddings are offset by one position, ensures that predictions for position $i$ can depend only on known outputs at positions less than $i$, preserving the auto-regressive property.

**Scaled Dot-Product Attention.** The fundamental building block maps a set of **queries**, **keys**, and **values** to an output. Each token's hidden representation is linearly projected three times to produce a query vector $q$ (what this token is looking for), a key vector $k$ (what this token advertises to others), and a value vector $v$ (the information this token provides when attended to). Stacking these across all tokens in the sequence gives matrices $Q$, $K$ (both of dimension $n \times d_k$), and $V$ (of dimension $n \times d_v$). The attention output is a weighted sum of the value vectors, where the weight on each value is determined by the dot-product compatibility between the corresponding query and key:

<div align="center">
![Figure 4: Attention](/figures/llm-fundamental-attention.png)
</div>

$$
\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V
$$

<div align="center">
![Figure 5: Self Attention](/figures/llm-fundamental-self-attention.png)
</div>

<div align="center">
![Figure 6: Self Attention Walkthrough](/figures/llm-fundamental-self-attention-walkthrough.png)
</div>

The scaling factor $1/\sqrt{d_k}$ prevents dot products from growing large in magnitude. If the components of $q$ and $k$ are independent with mean 0 and variance 1, their dot product has variance $d_k$ — without scaling, large $d_k$ pushes softmax into saturated regions with vanishingly small gradients.

**Multi-Head Attention.** A single attention head averages over all positions, which can prevent the model from jointly attending to information in different representation subspaces. To address this, the Transformer projects $Q$, $K$, $V$ into $h$ different subspaces and runs attention in parallel:

$$
\text{MultiHead}(Q, K, V) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1, \ldots, \text{head}_h) W^O
$$

$$
\text{where } \text{head}_i = \text{Attention}(QW_i^Q, KW_i^K, VW_i^V)
$$

<div align="center">
![Figure 7: MultiHead Attention](/figures/llm-fundamental-multihead-attention.png)
</div>

<div align="center">
![Figure 8: MultiHead Attention Walkthrough](/figures/llm-fundamental-multihead-attention-example.png)
</div>

With $h = 8$ heads and $d_k = d_v = d_{\text{model}} / h = 64$, the total computational cost is similar to single-head full-dimensionality attention — we simply trade width for breadth. Different heads can learn complementary attention patterns: one might capture syntactic dependencies, another semantic similarity, another local positional proximity.

It helps to track the tensor shapes. Start with token embeddings $X \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times D}$, where $N$ is the sequence length and $D = d_{\text{model}}$ is the embedding dimension. Linear projections produce $Q = XW^Q$, $K = XW^K$, and $V = XW^V$, each initially of shape $N \times (H \cdot D_H)$ where $H$ is the number of heads and $D_H = D / H$ is the dimension per head. Reshaping splits these into separate heads: $Q, K, V \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times N \times D_H}$.

For a single head $h$, $Q_h$ and $K_h$ have shape $N \times D_H$, so the attention score matrix is:

$$
E_h = Q_h K_h^T \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}
$$

because $(N \times D_H)(D_H \times N) = N \times N$. Entry $E_h[i, j]$ is the compatibility between token $i$ as query and token $j$ as key — row $i$ answers how much token $i$ should attend to every other token. The $D_H$ dimension disappears because the dot product sums over the feature dimension; what remains is a pairwise token-to-token similarity matrix. With $H$ heads, we get $H$ such matrices stacked as $E \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times N \times N}$. For example, with $H = 8$, $N = 10$, and $D_H = 64$, each head produces a $10 \times 10$ score matrix, and the full tensor has shape $8 \times 10 \times 10$. Softmax is applied over the key dimension (the last axis) so that each query token's weights over all keys sum to 1.

The Transformer uses multi-head attention in three ways: (1) **encoder self-attention** where all of $Q$, $K$, $V$ come from the previous encoder layer; (2) **masked decoder self-attention** where future positions are masked by setting their scores to $-\infty$ before softmax; and (3) **encoder-decoder cross-attention** where queries come from the decoder and keys/values come from the encoder output.

**Position-Wise Feed-Forward Network.** Each layer also contains a two-layer FFN applied independently to each position:

$$
\text{FFN}(x) = \max(0, \; xW_1 + b_1) W_2 + b_2
$$

The inner dimension is $d_{ff} = 2048$ while the input/output dimension is $d_{\text{model}} = 512$.

**Positional Encoding.** Self-attention is a set operation — it computes pairwise dot products between vectors and produces weighted sums, but nothing in the computation depends on the *order* of those vectors. If you permute the input tokens, the output is permuted in exactly the same way; the model cannot distinguish "The cat sat" from "sat cat The." This is fundamentally different from RNNs and convolutions, where position is implicit in the sequential or sliding-window structure. To break this permutation equivariance, the Transformer adds a **positional encoding** to each token's embedding before it enters the attention layers — a deterministic signal that is a function of the token's index, so that permuted variants are no longer identical inputs to the model. The original paper uses sinusoidal encodings:

$$
PE_{(pos, 2i)} = \sin\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i / d_{\text{model}}}}\right), \quad PE_{(pos, 2i+1)} = \cos\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i / d_{\text{model}}}}\right)
$$

The wavelengths form a geometric progression from $2\pi$ to $10000 \cdot 2\pi$. The key property is that for any fixed offset $k$, $PE_{pos+k}$ can be represented as a linear function of $PE_{pos}$, allowing the model to learn relative-position attention patterns.

**Why Self-Attention?** Compared to recurrence and convolution, self-attention offers:

* $O(1)$ maximum path length between any two positions (vs. $O(n)$ for RNNs, $O(n/k)$ for convolutions), making long-range dependencies easier to learn;
* full parallelizability with $O(1)$ sequential operations (vs. $O(n)$ for RNNs);
* $O(n^2 \cdot d)$ computational complexity per layer, which is faster than the $O(n \cdot d^2)$ of recurrent layers when $n < d$ — the common case with subword tokenization.

The trade-off is that the $O(n^2)$ term becomes expensive for very long sequences, motivating later work on sparse and linear attention variants.
